Quantum States

The Number Line Analogy

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \end{pmatrix} \]

Infinite possible positions spaced distance $dx$ apart
Imagine a quantum particle's state as a position on this line. Represent its state in a vector

For those of you who are familiar with calculus, you'll recognize this as a differential (infinitely small) distance
\[ |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
\[ |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \]
Remember these? We said we'd use them.

These vectors represent a quantum state (a qubit, a quantum bit) which can have either 0 or 1. Like bits form classical computers, qubits form quantum computers. The 0 or 1 can be represented by the spin of a particle like an electron (explain spin) and can be mainipulated by precise microwave pulses

These are simpler than the more complex number line we saw earlier, and thankfully we won't be dealing too much with state vectors of higher dimensions

Multi-Qubit States

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
\[ \begin{pmatrix} a_0 \\ a_1 \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_0 b_0 \\ a_0 b_1 \\ a_1 b_0 \\ a_1 b_1 \end{pmatrix} \]
We use the tensor product to describe two quantum states together
