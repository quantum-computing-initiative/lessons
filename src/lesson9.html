Quantum Computing

1980: Paul Benioff's paper discussing the possibility of a quantum computer

1981: Feynman claims that quantum computers can outperform classical

1992: Deustch-Jozsa algorithm developed

1994: Shor's Algorithm developed

1996: Grover's Algorithm developed

Feynman: "nature isn't classical, dammit, and if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical."

The largest number Shor's Algorithm has factored is 21. Our quantum computers don't have enough qubits to factor large numbers. Nevertheless, since cryptography depends on prime numbers being hard to factor, security researchers are looking into ways to make our systems more secure with quantum.

2011: D-Wave builds 1st quantum computer

2019: Google Quantum Supremacy

D-Wave computers have a lot more qubits, but they are not general-purpose quantum computers. They are for problems where graph interconnectedness (the degrees of the nodes) are relatively small -- only works in special cases, such as dealing with a game of checkers, for example. Also suitable for machine learning applications.

Quantum supremacy, loosely defined, is the point at which quantum computers solve a problem faster than any classical computer could reasonably be expected to solve.
