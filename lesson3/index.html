Intro to QM

Double Slit Experiment

\[ i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} |\psi(t) \rangle = \hat{H} |\psi(t) \rangle \]

Pg. 105-106 in Mannucci, Yanofsky Is light a wave or particle? This experiment can be done one photon at a time. One photon can go through both slits at the same time—superposition—and even cancel itself out. We can do this experiment with small particles—not just light Pattern on the detector and its relation to the wave function Don't worry, you won't really see this equation later. Understanding it is beyond the scope of our course, but this wave function determines our quantum state. Copenhagen interpretation of QM

Quantum States

\[ |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
\[ |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \]
\[ |\psi \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} \]
These vectors represent a quantum state, which can have either 0 or 1. $|\psi \rangle$ represents a superposition state

Superposition

\[ |\psi \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ \tfrac{i}{\sqrt{2}} \end{pmatrix} \]
Our vector $\psi$ is in a superposition of 0 and 1 Draw an analogy to how a photon can go through 2 slits at once Our amplitudes can be complex numbers Amplitudes are like the amplitudes in a wave

Observer Effect

Observing a system changes its state

Causes a superposition to collapse

Blind person trying to locate a ball with a stick analogy Schrodinger's Cat This is not the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Probabilities

\[ |\psi \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ \tfrac{i}{\sqrt{2}} \end{pmatrix} \]
$p(|x\rangle) = | \langle x| \psi \rangle|^2$
From Qiskit Textbook: Representing Quantum States and Mannucci, Yanofsky p. 96 To find the probability of finding our system in a particular state, we square the absolute amplitude of that state Show math on Jamboard Conservation of probability (sum of amplitudes squared must add to 1) We need complex numbers as when we square the complex amplitudes $a$ and $b$, sometimes these probabilities cancel themselves out This is like the interference in the double slit experiment
\[ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \]
\[ \begin{pmatrix} a_0 \\ a_1 \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} b_0 \\ b_1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_0 b_0 \\ a_0 b_1 \\ a_1 b_0 \\ a_1 b_1 \end{pmatrix} \]
We use the tensor product to describe two quantum states together Show math on Jamboard $a_0b_0$ shows the amplitude for both states being 0 Recall that the 0th element tells us the probability of the state being a 0. The 1st element tells us the probability of 0

Entangled States

\[ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \]

Cannot be factored!

Recall that we cannot factor entangled states The states only make sense together When one state collapses, the other will collapse instantaneously (even if the two quantum systems are very far apart) Show math on Jamboard
